## The kata

### Step 1: Read before you write

Start safe. Ask your tool to read your calendar:

<div class="tool-step" data-step="verify"></div>

Verify the output against your actual calendar. Does it match? This builds trust without any risk.

### Step 2: Create a simple event

```
"Block off 30 minutes of focus time on my calendar tomorrow at 2pm. No attendees, don't send any notifications."
```

Check your calendar - is it there? Right time? Right title?

### Step 3: Now try adding a video link

```
"Create a 30-minute meeting called 'Test Sync' tomorrow at 3pm with a Google Meet link. Invite me (your-email@company.com). Don't notify anyone."
```

Did it add a Meet link? Check the event - open it in your calendar and look.

> **Good to know:** The calendar API can auto-generate **Google Meet** links. But it can't create Zoom links - for Zoom you'd need to paste a link into the description or location yourself. Try asking: *"Add a Zoom link"* and see what it says.

### Step 4: Delete it

Ask the tool to delete the test events. Verify they're gone. Now you know the undo path before you need it.

### Step 5: Do it for real

Schedule a real meeting with a real attendee. But first say: **"Show me exactly what you'll create before sending it."** Review title, time, attendees, video link, description. Then approve.

<div class="tool-step" data-step="confirm-behavior"></div>

> **Watch out:** The default behavior is `sendUpdates: 'all'` - if you say "schedule a meeting with alice@company.com" it sends her a real invite immediately. To be safe, add *"don't send any notifications"* until you're ready.

## What to sit with

Did you check what it created, or just trust "Done"? Did it handle the video link the way you expected? Could you revoke access right now if you needed to? What would you do differently next time?

## What permissions you're granting

When you connect Google Calendar, here's what the tool gets:

- **Can:** Read all your events, create new events, delete events (permanent), invite people (sends real emails)
- **Cannot:** Access your contacts (you provide emails), read your email or files

## The bigger picture

You're giving an AI tool access to your Google Calendar - the ability to read events, create new ones, invite people, and delete meetings. That's real access with real consequences.

The skill you're building here isn't about calendars specifically. It's the pattern: connect an AI tool to a real service, understand what permissions you're granting, test safely before going live, and verify the result. This applies to every integration - Slack, GitHub, databases, email - not just calendars.
