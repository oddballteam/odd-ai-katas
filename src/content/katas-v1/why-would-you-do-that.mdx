## What's happening

You look at what the AI just did and something is wrong. Not a bug -- it works. But no experienced developer on your team would do it that way. It picked the wrong pattern, used the wrong abstraction, ignored a convention, or just made a choice that feels off.

That reaction is useful. Don't just fix it and move on. It's telling you one of two things:

**The AI doesn't know enough.** Your project instructions are missing something that you take for granted. A convention, a preference, a pattern. You know it because you've worked here. The AI doesn't because nobody wrote it down.

**The AI knows too much.** The conversation has been going for a while. Old errors, dead ends, abandoned approaches -- it's all still in the context. The AI is making bad decisions because it's confused, not because it's uninformed.

## The kata

### Figure out which one

Ask yourself: is this early in the session or deep into one?

**If it's early,** the AI is probably missing context. Try this:

```prompt
Why did you do it that way? What pattern were you following?
```

Listen to the answer. If it says "I chose this approach because..." and the reasoning is based on nothing in your codebase, that's a missing instruction.

Now write it down so it doesn't happen again:

```prompt
Add a note to CLAUDE.md that in this project, we always [the thing it should have known].
```

Some things worth putting in your instructions:
- Which patterns to follow (and where to find examples)
- Which libraries/approaches to prefer or avoid
- How your team structures components, tests, files
- Anything you've corrected the AI on more than once

**If it's deep into a session,** the context is probably the problem. The AI has been accumulating conversation history -- your prompts, its responses, errors, fixes, more errors. All of that is still in its working memory, competing for attention.

Try clearing the noise:

```prompt
/clear
```

Or check what's in context and trim it:

```prompt
/context
```

Or start a fresh session with your instructions intact:

```prompt
/init
```

After clearing, re-state what you're working on in one sentence and try again. If the quality jumps back up, the context was the problem.

## What to sit with

Every wince is a feedback loop. Early in a session, it means your instructions have a gap. Deep in a session, it means the context needs a reset. Either way, the fix takes 30 seconds and saves you from the same frustration next time.
