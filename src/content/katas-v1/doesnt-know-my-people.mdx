## What's happening

You connected your calendar. You connected your email. You gave the AI access to your tools. Then you said "set up a sync with Sarah about the API project" and it asked: "Who is Sarah? What's her email address?"

It feels like a gap. You gave it access to your *systems* but not your *world*. The AI can read your calendar and create events, but it doesn't know that Sarah Chen is your tech lead, that she's sarah.chen@company.com, that she's three hours ahead and prefers morning meetings, or that "the API project" is the thing your team has been working on since January.

Every time you start a new conversation, you're re-introducing your entire team.

## Why this is a problem worth naming

Most people assume that "connecting" an AI tool means it knows everything a human assistant would know. But connecting your calendar gives it *calendar operations* - read events, create events, check free/busy. It doesn't give it your contacts, your org chart, your team's working agreements, or the context that makes "schedule a sync with Sarah about the API project" a meaningful sentence.

This isn't a bug. It's a design boundary. But if you don't know it's there, you'll keep bumping into it and feeling like the tool is dumber than it should be.

## The scenario

> You're using an AI tool to help manage your week. You ask it to "block time for my 1:1 with Marcus on Thursday." It creates a calendar event called "1:1 with Marcus" - but it doesn't add Marcus as an attendee because it doesn't know who Marcus is. You provide his email. Next week you ask again. It asks for the email again. By the third week, you've started keeping Marcus's email in your clipboard.

## What to sit with

Before looking for solutions, notice the shape of the problem:

- How many people do you regularly schedule with? Five? Fifteen?
- How much context do you re-explain each session? Just emails, or also roles, timezones, project context?
- Would you *want* your AI to know your team? How much? Just names and emails, or also what they're working on, their preferences, their relationship to you?
- Where would you be comfortable storing that information - on your local machine? In the cloud? In a shared team file?

## The spectrum

People are handling this in different ways. There's no standard yet, just a range of approaches depending on how much effort you want to invest and how much you want the AI to know:

**Just tell it each time.** The simplest approach. "Schedule with sarah.chen@company.com." No setup, no privacy concerns, but repetitive.

**Let it remember.** Some AI tools have memory that persists across conversations. You say "Sarah Chen is my tech lead, sarah.chen@company.com, she's in EST and prefers morning meetings" once, and the tool remembers it next time. Claude has this. ChatGPT has this. It's lightweight but unstructured - and it's per-user, not shared with your team.

**Keep a local notes file.** Some people maintain a markdown file - `team.md`, `roster.md`, `people.md` - with their team's names, emails, roles, timezones, and current projects. Their AI tool reads this file at the start of each session. It's structured, private (stays on your machine), and easy to update. This is emerging as a pattern among people who use tools like Claude Code or connect their AI to an Obsidian vault.

**Use a shared team file.** Same idea, but checked into the repo or shared workspace so the whole team benefits. Some teams add a "people" section to their project context files (like CLAUDE.md) so the AI knows who owns what module, who to tag for reviews, and how to reach people.

**Connect a contacts source.** Some broader integrations (Google Workspace MCP servers, CRM connections) can give the AI access to your actual contacts database. This is the most complete solution but also the broadest permission - you're giving the AI access to potentially thousands of contacts, not just your team.

## The kata

Pick the person you schedule with most often. Right now, find out: does your AI tool know who they are? Ask it. If it doesn't, try one of the approaches above - whichever feels right for your comfort level. The goal isn't to adopt a system. It's to notice the gap and make a conscious choice about whether and how to close it.
