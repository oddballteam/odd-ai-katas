## What's happening

You're staring at a suggestion from your AI tool. It wants to refactor a function, reorganize some files, or rewrite a section of your code. It might even be a good suggestion. But there's a voice in the back of your head: *what if it messes things up and I can't get back?*

So you don't click accept. Or you copy-paste the original into a notepad "just in case." Or you only let the AI touch throwaway files, never anything important. The AI becomes a tool you use with one hand on the emergency brake.

## Why this is a problem worth naming

This isn't irrational caution - it's a reasonable response to not knowing your safety net. If you've never actually *undone* an AI's change, you don't have muscle memory for it. And without that muscle memory, every AI interaction carries a low-grade anxiety that adds up over a full day of work.

The good news is that you're almost never in a one-way-door situation. Modern AI tools and editors have multiple layers of undo, and once you've used them once or twice on purpose, the fear drops dramatically. The problem isn't that recovery is hard - it's that most people have never practiced it before they need it.

Here's what's actually available to you:

- **"Undo that"** - the simplest version. You can literally tell the AI "undo what you just did" or "revert that last change" and it will reverse its own work. Most people don't realize this works, but it does.
- **Claude Code's `/rewind`** - gives you a timeline of both code changes *and* conversation history. You can step back to any point, not just the last change. It's like a save-point system for your entire session.
- **VS Code's local history** - every time a file is saved, VS Code keeps a snapshot. Right-click a file → "Open Timeline" and you can click back to any prior version. This works regardless of what made the change.
- **Git** - the ultimate safety net underneath all of it. If you're committing regularly (or letting your AI tool commit for you), you can always get back to a known-good state.

## The scenario

> You're using an AI coding assistant to help restructure your API routes. It suggests moving handlers into separate files and updating all the imports. It's a big change - touching six files. You can see the diff and it looks reasonable, but you've been burned before by large refactors. You hesitate, then decide to just do it manually, one file at a time, so you can control each step. The AI sits idle while you spend forty minutes doing what it could have done in seconds.

## What to sit with

Before reaching for a solution, notice the pattern:

- When the AI suggests a change, what's the *first* thing you feel? Excitement about the result, or anxiety about the risk?
- Have you ever actually tried to undo an AI's change? Not in an emergency - just to see how it works?
- Do you know which recovery mechanism you'd reach for first if something went wrong right now?
- Is your caution proportional to the actual risk, or to the *unknown* of not having practiced recovery?

## The kata

Set up a low-stakes experiment. Open a file you don't care about - a scratch file, a personal project, anything where mistakes don't matter. Then do this:

**Round 1: Just say undo.** Ask your AI to make a change - rename a variable, add a function, restructure something. Let it. Then tell it: "undo that." Watch it reverse the change. Notice how simple that was.

**Round 2: Use your editor's history.** Ask the AI to make another change. This time, don't ask it to undo - go to your editor instead. In VS Code, right-click the file tab and choose "Open Timeline," then click a previous entry to restore it. In other editors, look for local history or file snapshots. The point is knowing this exists *before* you need it.

**Round 3: Use `/rewind` (Claude Code).** If you're using Claude Code, make a few changes through conversation. Then type `/rewind`. You'll see a timeline of everything - code changes and conversation turns. Step back to an earlier point. Notice that it doesn't just undo the code - it rewinds the conversation too, so the AI's context matches the code state.

**Round 4: The git safety net.** Make a commit. Then ask the AI to make a big, messy change - something you'd normally be afraid to try. Let it happen. Look at the result. Then run `git checkout .` to snap everything back to your last commit. Clean slate, zero damage.

The goal isn't to memorize four undo techniques. It's to do each one *once*, on purpose, so that the next time the AI suggests a big change, your gut reaction shifts from "what if I can't get back" to "I know exactly how to get back."
